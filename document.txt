Initial description:
Contrasting where a video action-recognition model “looks” to where the actor looked while performing the action, to test whether correct predictions rely on task-critical regions and whether the model’s focus leads or lags human gaze.

Goals
When the model predicts correctly, does its attention overlap with human gaze more?


When the model predicts incorrectly, is alignment worse?


Does model attention:


lead human gaze (model focuses earlier)?


lag human gaze (model focuses later)?

Slide Notes:
Objective:
Comparing where a video action-recognition model “looks” to where the actor looked while performing the action, to test whether correct predictions rely on task-critical regions and whether the model’s focus leads or lags human gaze.
Motivation:
We want to know whether a video action model’s evidence overlaps with the same task-relevant regions the actor fixates during execution, and how that overlap changes across actions and time. 
Approach:
Train or fine-tune a standard video action model on EGTEA Gaze+[1] labels without gaze. EGTEA provides paired action labels and gaze so we can measure this directly. Generate Grad-CAM maps, score alignment to actor gaze, then analyze alignment for correct vs incorrect predictions and across small time shifts to measure lead or lag.
